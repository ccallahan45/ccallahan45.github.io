About me

I am a PhD candidate and NSF Graduate Research Fellow at Dartmouth College in the EEES program, working with Dr. Justin Mankin and the Climate Modeling and Impacts Group. My research focuses on the economic and social consequences of climate change. I use a wide range of tools, from simple and complex climate models to econometric regression techniques, to understand the relationship between climate extremes and outcomes such as human health and economic growth. My previous work focused on the connections between climate variability and the stagnation patterns that produce poor air quality in Beijing, and my current work focuses on constraining the relationship between climate change and global economic growth.

I was a competitive debater in both high school and college, and I still work with the Dartmouth Forensic Union's policy debate team, coaching advocacy and argument construction. I'm also a fan of the Chicago Cubs, open science, and the Oxford comma.

Research interests

Economic effects of climate change

The economic effects of climate change could imperil growth in living standards for millions of people around the world, but the precise magnitude and persistence of these economic growth effects remains unclear. I have a number of ongoing projects that use econometric regression techniques and climate model simulations to both better understand the effects of rising temperatures on growth and link these effects back to individual nations' emissions. Projects include exploring the linkages between heat waves and subnational economic growth (currently in review) and understanding the effect of El Niño on tropical countries' economies (also in review). Another thread of this research uses custom simulations with a simple climate model to attribute economic changes from historical warming to individual nations' emissions. Legal liability for climate damages has often been limited by the lack of scientific research connecting individual emitters' emissions to the downstream effects of rising temperatures, so a framework for attributing these economic impacts to individual emitters could provide critical information to decision-makers.

El Niño and its impacts

El Niño is the primary mode of interannual variability in the Earth system, so it has immense influence on the global climate and, by extension, human well-being. Understanding how El Niño may change under warming is a high priority for climate science, but immense uncertainty remains due in part to internal climate variability. I led an international collaboration of scientists to use a new collection of millennial-scale climate model simulations to constrain the forced response of El Niño to increased CO2, recently published in Nature Climate Change. We found that there is substantial model disagreement in the next couple of centuries, but after hundreds of years, El Niño amplitude clearly and robustly decreases, in tandem with a more homogeneous Pacific. Our findings are consistent with paleoclimate evidence of a "permanent El Niño" in past high-CO2 climates. A current project of mine takes a slightly different tack, examining the effect of El Niño on economic growth and how such effects may change in the future. El Niño appears to persistently decrease national economic growth, especially in tropical countries, and anthropogenic changes to the global climate response to El Niño events may increase these economic penalties in the future.

Atmospheric stagnation and air pollution

Poor air quality can be incredibly damaging to human health. Episodes of poor air quality are partly determined by the emission of air pollutants, but these pollutants only accumulate in the near-surface environment when favorable meteorological conditions occur. These conditions include anticyclonic circulation and favorable wind directions, though the details differ by region. How might climate change affect these meteorological patterns? My first two papers examined the influence of climate warming on the occurrence of these favorable meteorological conditions near Beijing, China. My first paper, in the Journal of Geophysical Research - Atmospheres, used extreme event attribution techniques to show that warming to date had only a small influence on the occurrence of extreme air pollution in China's January 2013 "airpocalypse." My second paper, in Geophysical Research Letters, showed that internal climate variability plays a significant role in projections of the anticyclonic circulation patterns that govern the accumulation of these harmful pollutants.
